**Primary user — "The serious teenage athlete" (Maya, 16):**
Competitive youth soccer player balancing varsity + club + school + family logistics. Ambitious but can't drop $20k/yr on a premier club. Owns a phone, not a Whoop. Wants to improve tangibly every week. Distrusts apps that "gamify" or lecture. Wants the app to respect them as a serious athlete. Will uninstall anything that feels patronizing or hype-y.

*Design implication:* Confident typography; no cartoonish icons; no badges/points/streaks as the primary motivator; plain-English data with no medical over-promise.

**Primary buyer — "The supportive parent" (Nicole, 42):**
Wants to support without becoming the coach. Budget-conscious. Values clarity over charts. Trusts brands that don't over-promise. Deciding factor: "does this take pressure off me without taking over?"

*Design implication:* About page, pricing page, and footer are for her. Honest language ("AI is useful, labeled, bounded"). Clean pricing — $12/mo is easy to explain. No "enterprise" dark patterns.

**Program decision maker — "The access-focused coach/program lead" (Mr. Álvarez, 38):**
Runs a school or community program. No six-figure software budget. Needs roster visibility, drill assignments, clip review, coach comments. Will champion Smartplay if a pilot proves a "useful first week."

*Design implication:* Features page's role architecture section speaks to him. Pricing page's "Team Pilot" + "Community Program" paths honor that his decision needs a conversation, not a self-serve checkout. Contact page is mailto-first with specific pilot intake fields.

**Conversion leading-the-eye targets:**
1. Primary CTA everywhere: "Start the 14-day trial" → `/signup`
2. Secondary: "See the week connect" (home), "View live plan" (features), "Plan a pilot" (contact), "Log in" (header)
3. Anti-CTA clarity: pricing clearly labels which plans are live vs pilot-only — builds trust, reduces drop-off
